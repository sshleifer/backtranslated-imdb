This 3-hour epic (seems much shorter) explores the will to power and conquest and the contradictory motives underlying this quest by following two parallel lives: Emperor Q'in, whose desire to unite Chinese feudal states has its foundation in noble goals but develops in violent oppression, isolation and ultimate helplessness; and the killer Jing Ke, a mercenary murderer who comes to recognize the unintended consequences of the murder and finds a form of salvation. As with all great art, it can be understood on many levels. The film evoked images and ideas from Homer, Euripid, Machiavelli, Shakespeare and Freud for me. The story itself is true (I checked it on britannica.com!), So this movie will appeal to history enthusiasts. Ironically, Q'in spent most of his last years in vain search for immortality (not shown on screen, but in line with the chart). The film can also be portrayed as an allegory of Maoism and the Cultural Revolution. Through the film, it can be understood that the last 50 years of Chinese history have had its precedent over the past 3000 years. Fortunately for the audience, these complex ideas are being developed in a film that is rich in images. , action and pagent. The battle scenes themselves are worth the admission.
