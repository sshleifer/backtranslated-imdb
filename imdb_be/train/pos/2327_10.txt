14-year-old girl develops his first serious crush on the 17-year-old boy who lives next door, at the same time trying to overcome their feelings of inferiority compared to her older sister. It's a simple premise of this beautiful poetic coming of age film directed by Robert Mulligan. Mulligan previously known direction of the summer 42 in 1971 and To Kill a Mockingbird in 1962, the two giants of the genre elderly. Here he directs newcomers starring Reese Witherspoon, in her film debut as a 14-year-old girl; Emily Warfield, like an older sister; Jason London, as well as the court, the 17-year-old boy. Reese Witherspoon is surprisingly good in his debut film, showing all the emotions that the 14-year-old girl feels the experience of young love and pain, he never hits a false note. Warfield and London are both equally good as well. The film accurately reflects the thoughts and feelings of every teenager in respect of love with heart sensitivity, never crossing in excess of sentimentality, even once. Kudos autobiographical script with Jenny Wingfield; it is one of the very few films about young love, honest and consistent in tone, without being emotionally dishonest or sensationalism. Music is surprisingly simple, emphasizing the tone and mood of the scene to the next, but never becomes intrusive. Beautiful cinematography is famous horror director Freddie Francis, who was in his 70s when it was filmed. Tess Harper and Sam Waterston parents play with dead accuracy girls Strive to 1957, care, strict, and just emotionally. Gail Strickland is good, and how the boy's mother. There are feelings to sort through, lessons to learn the truth and to face in this sweet-natured film that packs an emotional wallop. To date, this is the last film by Robert Mulligan.
