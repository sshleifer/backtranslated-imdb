This delightful turgid melodrama represents Douglas Sirk at his highest tense. It scares the soft wistfulness of "All That Heaven Allows" and the pristine sentimentality of "Imitation of Life" and instead goes for feverish anxiety and overheating tension. And of course it's all trapped in live Technicolor. The Cornball story has something to do with a friendship between Rock Hudson and Robert Stack, who becomes a rivalry when Hudson cheats Lauren Bacall's feelings, but who is really aware of the story? Dorothy Malone won a Best Supporting Actress Academy Award for her outstanding performance as Stack's sister, taking the family business into her hands when no one else wants. A highlight of the movie comes when this highly sprouted wild child breaks into a hectic dance in her bedroom that cannot bear the limitations placed on her by the middle class decency. As so often happens in Sirk films, the scene is both inadvertently hilarious in its absurdity and yet strangely moving in its effectiveness. Sirk came closer than anyone else to turning pure camp into high art and satisfying the Philistines and the high brow at the same time within the same film.
